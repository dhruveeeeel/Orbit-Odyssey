Earth History

Formation

A 2012 artistic impression of the early Solar System's protoplanetary disk from which Earth and other Solar System bodies were formed.

The oldest material in the Solar System is dated to 4.5682+0.0002 −0.0004 Ga (billion years) ago.

By 4.54±0.04 Ga, the primordial Earth had formed. The bodies in the Solar System evolved with the Sun.

The nebular theory suggests that planets formed from a spinning, flattened circumstellar disk within a solar nebula.

Planetesimals formed by accretion, and the primordial Earth likely took 70 to 100 million years to form.

The Moon's formation is hypothesized to be the result of a collision with a Mars-sized object named Theia.

Between 4.1 and 3.8 Ga, asteroid impacts during the Late Heavy Bombardment altered the Moon's and Earth's surfaces.

After Formation

Earth's atmosphere and oceans formed through volcanic activity and outgassing.

Water vapor condensed into oceans, augmented by water and ice from asteroids, protoplanets, and comets.

Earth's magnetic field, established by 3.5 Ga, prevented atmospheric loss to solar wind.

The first solid crust formed as the molten outer layer cooled. Continental crust evolved through plate tectonics.

Supercontinents like Rodinia, Pannotia, and Pangaea formed and broke apart over millions of years.

The most recent ice ages began about 40 Ma, intensifying during the Pleistocene about 3 Ma.

Glaciation cycles repeated approximately every 21,000, 41,000, and 100,000 years.

Origin of Life and Evolution

Chemical reactions led to the first self-replicating molecules around four billion years ago.

The last common ancestor of all current life forms emerged half a billion years later.

Photosynthesis evolved, leading to the accumulation of molecular oxygen and the development of eukaryotes.

Life colonized Earth's surface, with evidence of microbial fossils dating back 3.45 billion years.

The Neoproterozoic era (1000 to 539 Ma) may have experienced a "Snowball Earth" covered in ice.

After the Cambrian explosion (535 Ma), five major mass extinctions occurred, including the one 66 Ma triggered by an asteroid impact.

Mammalian life diversified over the past 66 million years, leading to the evolution of humans.

Future

Earth's long-term future is tied to the Sun's evolution.

Over the next 1.1 billion years, solar luminosity will increase, affecting the carbon cycle and potentially leading to oxygen loss.

Earth's mean temperature may reach 100 °C in 1.5 billion years, leading to ocean water evaporation.

The Sun will evolve into a red giant in about 5 billion years, potentially impacting Earth's orbit.

Earth's fate, whether it moves to a distant orbit or vaporizes, depends on tidal effects during the Sun's expansion.
